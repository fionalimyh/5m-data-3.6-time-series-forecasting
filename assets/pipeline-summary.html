STAGE 01

Introduction to Time-Series Data

Time-series data is a sequence of points collected at ordered intervals — as opposed to the cross-sectional data used earlier in the course. The lesson opens by naming the three components that any series can show:

Trend

A long-term increase or decrease, not necessarily linear — it can even change direction partway through (e.g. antidiabetic drug sales).

Seasonal

A pattern tied to a fixed, known calendar period — time of year, day of week — such as year-end pricing shifts in drug sales.

Cyclic

Rises and falls with no fixed frequency, usually driven by the business cycle, typically lasting 2+ years.

Cyclic ≠ seasonal. If the frequency is unchanging and tied to the calendar, it's seasonal; if not, it's cyclic. Cycles also tend to run longer, and vary more in magnitude, than seasonal patterns.

STAGE 02

Visualizing Time-Series Data

A sequence of diagnostic plots for reading structure by eye before fitting anything — culminating in a test for whether a series is just noise.

Time series plot

The base view: time on the x-axis, the variable of interest on the y-axis.

Seasonal plot

Assesses whether seasonality is present, and what shape it takes.

Subseries plot

Compares the seasonal pattern across different years side by side.

Lag plot

Checks for autocorrelation — a random series shows no identifiable pattern.

ACF / correlogram

Plots ACF(k) — the correlation between a series and its own lag k — over lags 1..20, with a shaded 95% confidence band (alpha=0.05); values outside it are statistically significant.

White noise check

A series is white noise if its mean ≈ 0, its variance is constant over time, and its autocorrelation is ≈ 0 at every lag.

STAGE 03

Time Series Decomposition

Splits a series into trend (T), seasonal (S) and residual (R) components, so each influence can be measured on its own.

Additive  ·  Yt = Tt + St + Rt

Use when the seasonal swing stays roughly constant in size as the series grows.

Multiplicative  ·  Yt = Tt × St × Rt

Use when the seasonal swing grows in proportion to the level of the series.

Reading the residual

After removing trend and seasonality, whatever is left should look random with a mean close to zero — anything structured left behind means the decomposition missed something.

STAGE 04

Modelling Evaluation Framework

Because observation order carries information, time series can't use a random train/test split — the rules below preserve that order, and apply identically to every model in Stage 05.

Temporal train/test split

Train on the earlier portion, test on what comes after — stops future information from leaking into training.

Time series cross-validation

Sliding window moves a fixed-size training window forward in time; expanding window keeps growing it to include everything seen so far. More robust performance estimates, at the cost of extra compute and a harder window/horizon choice.

Every model below is scored on the same five metrics:

MAE

Mean absolute error — average size of the miss, direction ignored.

MSE

Mean squared error — squares the errors, so outliers dominate.

RMSE

√MSE — brings the error back into the original units.

MAPE

Mean absolute percentage error — scale-free, undefined at zero.

sMAPE

Symmetric MAPE — fixes MAPE's zero-division and its asymmetric penalty.

STAGE 05

Forecasting Models

The Stage 04 framework is applied identically across three competing model families, each fit on the same Box–Jenkins airline-passengers series, from simplest baseline to fully automated selection.

Baseline

Naive Forecasting

Assumes the most recent observation is the best predictor — the floor every other model has to beat.

Naive  ŷt+1 = yt

Tomorrow equals today.

Seasonal naive  ŷt+1 = yt+1−m

Repeat the value from the same point last season.

Drift

Extrapolates the average rate of change seen across the whole history.

Classical

ARIMA family

Requires a stationary series (constant mean/variance/autocovariance), checked with the ADF test and enforced through differencing — the "I" in ARIMA.

ARIMA(p, d, q)

p AR lags of the series, d differences to reach stationarity, q lagged forecast-error terms.

SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q,m)

Adds a seasonal AR/I/MA triple over period m for data with both trend and seasonality.

Reading ACF/PACF

PACF cuts off at lag p (AR order); ACF cuts off at lag q (MA order); both tailing off gradually signals a mixed ARMA process.

AutoARIMA

Searches (p,d,q) combinations automatically, scored by AIC/BIC.

Worked on the airline series: d=1 removed the trend, D=1 (m=12) removed yearly seasonality; ACF/PACF spikes at lags 1 and 12 pointed to AR(1)/MA(1) plus seasonal AR(1)/MA(1).

Smoothing

Exponential Smoothing / ETS

Weighted averages of past observations, with weights decaying exponentially — each method below adds one more component the last one lacked.

SES

One smoothing constant α; assumes no trend, no seasonality.

Holt's linear trend

Adds a trend equation (β*) on top of SES's level equation.

Holt-Winters

Adds a seasonal equation (γ, period m) on top of Holt's method.

ETS state-space

Names each model by a 3-letter Error/Trend/Season code, each Additive, Multiplicative or None — e.g. ANN, MAM.

AutoETS

Searches all ETS component combinations, scored by AIC/BIC/HQIC.
